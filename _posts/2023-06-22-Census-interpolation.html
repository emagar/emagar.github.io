The longitudinal analysis of elections is richer when socio-demographic controls are included, such as voting age population or the share of indigenous language speakers in a unit. A key source of aggregate data is the census. Yet the mismatch between decennial censuses and the three-year election cycle is an obstacle. The census bureau fills the gap partially, distributing annual municipal projections of selected indicators only (excluding voting age population, see this for detail).

Systematic analysis of municipalities large and small (N ≈ 2500), of less common units like federal or state electoral districts, or of much more disaggregated units like secciones electorales (N ≈ 67k) necessitates self-estimation of off-census socio-demographic indicators from official data. This note discusses one approach to achieve this.

1 Filling gaps through projection

The problem, for illustrative purposes, is obtaining sección-level voting age populations for triennial federal elections from 1994 to 2021. In the period, INEGI distributes population counts for 1990, 2000, 2010, and 2020 (General Censuses) and mid-decade counts in 1995 and 2005.1 From here on, I use the term census to refer to counts and censuses indistinctly.

I proceed with inter- and extra-polation to project socio-demographic indicators for non-census years from the census/conteo figures available. Three approaches are inspected here:

  • Line segments connecting each unit's contiguous pair of census points;
  • A linear regression of census points for the unit on time; and
  • A log-linear regression of the (log-transformed) points on time.

A further obstacle is that the census bureau began distribution of sección-level census aggregates only in 2005.2 Whereas five census points are available for municipalities (1995 2000 2005 2010 2020), there are only three for secciones (2005 2010 2020).

2 Three principles

  1. Projection requires at least two different census points, to infer change.
  2. Longitudinal projection becomes less reliable the further you move away from a census point.
  3. Therefore, the more census points the better the projection.

3 Assumptions

  1. While population counts are discrete variables, they are modeled as continuous variables.
  2. Each approach has a demographic trend model: the procession is straight in the segment and linear projection approaches, and curvilinear in the log-linear approach.

4 The segments model

Formally, letting \(T_1, T_2, \dots\) be contiguous census years, a given unit's population in year \(t\) in the segment model is:

\[\text{pop}_t = \text{pop}_{T_1} + \frac{\text{pop}_{T_2} - \text{pop}_{T_1}}{T_2 - T_1} \times (t - T_1) \forall t \in (T_1,T_2)\]

\[\text{pop}_t = \text{pop}_{T_2} + \frac{\text{pop}_{T_3} - \text{pop}_{T_2}}{T_3 - T_2} \times (t - T_2) \forall t \in (T_2,T_3)\]

\[\text{pop}_t = \left\{ & \text{pop}_{T_1} + \frac{\text{pop}_{T_2} - \text{pop}_{T_1}}{T_2 - T_1} \times (t - T_1) \forall t \in (T_1,T_2) \\ & \text{pop}_{T_2} + \frac{\text{pop}_{T_3} - \text{pop}_{T_2}}{T_3 - T_2} \times (t - T_2) \forall t \in (T_2,T_3) \right\]

Figure 1 below illustrates segments projection with two municipalities in the northern state of Coahuila. Progreso municipality had nearly flat voting-age population growth in the period. Pay attention to circles in the plot only, black is for census values, white for projections. Fitting separate linear segments for each inter-census period accommodates changing trends of demographic change: a slight dip is visible between 1995 and 2010.
